What are fish farms?

Fish farming, also known as aquaculture, is the practice of raising fish in controlled environments, including tanks, enclosures, or offshore pens, designed to meet the growing global demand for seafood. Originally developed to reduce the strain on wild fish populations and create a steady, reliable food source, fish farms are now common worldwide. They are seen as a solution to overfishing and as a way to stabilize seafood supply chains, particularly for high-demand species such as tuna and salmon. However, the practices within fish farming have raised several environmental, ethical, and health concerns.

In Malta, the fish farming industry has grown significantly, with most operations focused on high-value species, especially bluefin tuna, intended for export. While fish farms have helped Malta participate in the global seafood trade and created local employment, the environmental and social consequences have stirred public debate. The confined conditions in Maltese fish farms can generate waste and pollution that impact nearby marine ecosystems and create concerns about the spread of disease to native species. As fish farms grow in scale, they continue to influence both the local environment and the quality of seafood available in Malta.

Animal Welfare

Animal welfare in fish farming remains one of the most pressing ethical issues within the aquaculture industry. Fish farms often involve dense populations of fish in restricted environments, which can lead to chronic stress, physical injuries, and increased susceptibility to diseases and parasites. High stocking densities mean fish have limited space, which impedes natural behaviors and can cause aggression or injury among individuals. Furthermore, farmed fish are often subjected to handling and transport methods that can lead to further stress and mortality, which impacts not only the health of individual fish but the overall quality of the harvest.

In Malta, animal welfare within fish farms is a growing concern, especially given the country’s focus on bluefin tuna—a species requiring large swimming spaces and often housed in confined sea cages. Local welfare advocates argue that the industry needs stricter regulations to ensure fish are treated humanely and that living conditions meet welfare standards. The Maltese aquaculture industry is encouraged to adopt practices such as reducing stocking densities, improving water quality, and using more humane handling techniques. By prioritizing welfare, Malta could enhance the quality of farmed fish while also addressing ethical concerns related to the treatment of fish in these facilities.
